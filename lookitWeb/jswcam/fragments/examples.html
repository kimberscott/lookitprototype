With younger children and infants, we often design our studies to use children's looking behavior rather than verbal responses.

In the "Learning New Verbs" study, a replication of \ Yuan and Fisher (2009) , we first have children find a few familiar verbs on the screen:

For the second verb the parent is asked to close his/her eyes so as not to influence the child:

Children then watch a brief video where two women introduce a made-up verb like 'daxing.' Some children hear a transitive verb (one that takes an object, like "She daxed the table") and some hear an intransitive verb ("She daxed").

Afterwards, children see two actions on the screen: an action with just one participant and an action with two participants.

Children who heard a transitive verb are more likely to look at the action with two participants when asked to "find daxing." But this effect vanishes in a control condition where the child is simply asked "what's happening?"

Of course, children may choose to look at particular scenes for many different reasons. Over many children, though, these effects average out and we can look for differences caused just by the experimental condition.
